The Science

MSCs: Mesenchymal stem/stromal cells

"Mesenchymal stem/stromal cells (MSCs) are a type of adult stem cell found in various tissues, including bone marrow, fat (adipose tissue), umbilical cord, and others. They are known for their ability to differentiate into multiple cell types, secrete bioactive molecules that help modulate immune responses, reduce inflammation, and support tissue repair—making them attractive for regenerative medicine, and home to sites of injury, meaning they can migrate toward areas of tissue damage or inflammation.

MSCs are not as pluripotent as embryonic stem cells, but they have significant therapeutic potential due to their regenerative and immunomodulatory properties. They’re currently being researched and tested in clinical trials for conditions like osteoarthritis, graft-versus-host disease, myocardial infarction, and autoimmune diseases.

Mesenchymal stem/stromal cells (MSCs) are being actively studied as a potential therapy for Parkinson’s disease l, although they are not yet a standard treatment. Their therapeutic potential in PD comes primarily from their neuroprotective and immunomodulatory effects rather than directly replacing lost dopaminergic neurons. Here’s how MSCs might help:

1. Neuroprotection: MSCs secrete trophic factors like BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor), GDNF (glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor), and VEGF (vascular endothelial growth factor), which protects remaining dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra, promotes neuron survival and function, and reduces oxidative stress and apoptosis.

2. Immunomodulation: PD involves chronic neuroinflammation. MSCs can reduce inflammatory cytokines (e.g., TNF-α, IL-1β), shift microglia from a pro-inflammatory to a neuroprotective state, and promote a more balanced immune environment in the brain

3. Stimulation of Endogenous Repair, by modulating the brain’s microenvironment, MSCs may stimulate endogenous neural stem cells, support repair processes in the brain

4. Delivery Methods: MSCs can be administered via intravenous injection (systemic effect, but limited CNS penetration), intrathecal injection (into cerebrospinal fluid), or direct brain injection (into the striatum or substantia nigra—more invasive)

5. Limitations and Challenges: MSCs do not naturally differentiate into dopaminergic neurons in vivo at clinically meaningful levels, their effects may be temporary, requiring repeated administrations, and safety has generally been good, but long-term efficacy is still under investigation.

6. Current Status: there are ongoing clinical trials assessing MSCs in PD. Early-phase studies suggest they are safe and well-tolerated, with some reports of improved motor symptoms and quality of life—but large, controlled trials are still needed.
